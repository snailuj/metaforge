Pipeline Optimization for Computational Metaphor Generation and Ranking
The generation, matching, and ranking of metaphorical mappings represent one of the most structurally complex challenges in computational linguistics. The phenomenon of metaphor fundamentally defies standard distributional semantics; it relies on the systematic violation of literal semantic boundaries while preserving deep structural or relational isomorphisms. The architecture under review—a property-based matching engine operating over 20k WordNet synsets enriched with LLM-extracted features mapped to a 35k-entry curated vocabulary—has encountered a severe performance plateau. The observed degradation in Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) from 0.034 to 0.025 following the clustering of synonymous properties (e.g., mapping "heavy" and "weighty" to a single identifier) highlights a critical theoretical misalignment between the engineering approach and the cognitive mechanics of figurative language.
Clustering synonymous properties to enable fuzzy matching artificially compresses the dimensionality of the feature space. Metaphorical aptness does not rely on broad, fuzzy semantic overlap. Instead, it hinges on the precise intersection of highly salient, distinctive features. Deduplication flattens the salience distribution, destroying the "long-tail" conceptual signals that differentiate an apt metaphorical vehicle from a generic, literal, or anomalous classification. To transcend the current limitations and elevate the MRR, the pipeline must evolve beyond simple attributional overlap, inverse document frequency (IDF) weighting, and symmetric distance metrics. This comprehensive analysis deconstructs the state-of-the-art in computational metaphor generation, aptness scoring, cross-domain similarity, feature norms, and alternative psycholinguistic signals, explicitly identifying theoretical models and engineering techniques that must be adapted to optimize the existing architecture.
1. Metaphor Generation Systems
An analysis of existing metaphor generation systems reveals a transition from purely rule-based, property-theoretic frameworks to hybrid neural architectures that incorporate symbolic knowledge graphs, clustering, and discriminative decoding.

	High
	Utilizing a symbolic knowledge graph (ConceptNet) to bridge abstract concepts and literal expressions, combined with a metaphor discriminator during decoding.

	https://aclanthology.org/2021.naacl-main.336.pdf
	The MERMAID system generates metaphors by transforming literal utterances into metaphorical ones, specifically focusing on verb replacement. The architecture employs BART, fine-tuned on a parallel corpus of 93,498 literal-metaphorical sentence pairs extracted from the Gutenberg Poetry corpus. The fundamental innovation lies in its semantic mapping phase. MERMAID leverages COMET, a language model fine-tuned on ConceptNet, to establish symbolic equivalence. For example, if a literal verb evokes a symbolic connection to the concept of "love," the generator is constrained to select metaphorical verbs that evoke the identical symbol. To counteract the innate bias of language models toward high-frequency literal text, MERMAID employs a metaphor discriminator during the decoding process to actively steer token selection.
The current pipeline's reliance on LLM-extracted properties snapped to a curated vocabulary can be significantly enhanced by adopting MERMAID's symbolic mapping. By passing both the target synset and candidate vehicle synsets through a ConceptNet-based relational model, the pipeline can score pairs based on the distance between their generated symbolic nodes rather than requiring direct property string overlap. Furthermore, the discriminative decoding concept can be adapted as a post-generation filter. A classification model trained on a known metaphor dataset can re-rank the candidate vehicles generated by the property-matching engine, penalizing candidates that share too much literal context with the target domain.

	High
	Identifying conceptual mappings through bipartite verb clustering and noun clustering based on distributional contexts.

	Shutova's framework represents a landmark in unsupervised metaphor identification and generation. The algorithm begins with seed examples of metaphorical mappings, utilizing verb clustering to model the source domain and noun clustering to identify target domains. The Metaforge pipeline clustered the properties themselves to aid matching, which blurred the distinctiveness of the features and degraded the MRR. Shutova's system dictates that one must cluster the targets and vehicles themselves (the actual nouns and verbs) based on broad distributional contexts, not their internal diagnostic properties. The architecture should retain the exact, unclustered 35k-entry property vocabulary but implement clustering on the 20k WordNet synsets based on their macroscopic FastText embeddings. Metaphorical mappings can then be scored at the macro cluster-to-cluster level (Domain A to Domain B) before being evaluated at the specific, high-resolution synset-to-synset level.

2. Metaphor Quality and Aptness Scoring
The current scoring composite relies heavily on property count and IDF-weighted overlap. This approach is theoretically flawed for metaphor processing. Inverse Document Frequency weights terms based on their global rarity across a corpus. However, metaphorical aptness is governed by the localized salience of a property within the specific vehicle domain, regardless of its global rarity or frequency.

	Beyond Literal Similarity / Salience Imbalance (Ortony, 1979 / Tversky, 1977)
	Critical
	Modifying Tversky's Contrast Model to account for the asymmetric "salience imbalance" inherent in non-literal similarity.
	Replacing IDF-weighting entirely with a highly asymmetric Salience Imbalance multiplier favoring vehicle properties.
	https://www.ideals.illinois.edu/items/17751/bitstreams/63724/data.pdf
	The foundational mathematical approach to semantic similarity is Tversky’s Contrast Model, which posits that the similarity between a topic (a) and a vehicle (b) is a weighted function of their intersecting features minus their disjoint features: s(a,b) = \theta f(A \cap B) - \alpha f(A - B) - \beta f(B - A). However, Andrew Ortony’s seminal work recognized that this model applies accurately only to literal similarity, where f(A_n) \approx f(B_n) (the salience of the shared feature is roughly equal in both objects).
For non-literal similarity—metaphor—Ortony introduced the critical concept of Salience Imbalance. A high-quality metaphor requires that the shared attributes in the intersection (A \cap B) possess extremely high salience in the vehicle (B) but low, marginal, or latent salience in the topic (a). For example, in the metaphor "anger is a volcano," the property "erupts" is highly salient to the vehicle "volcano" but minimally salient in the literal, dictionary definition of the topic "anger." The metaphor functions specifically by projecting the high-salience ground of the vehicle onto the topic.
The IDF-weighted overlap in the current pipeline must be completely replaced. Global IDF penalizes common properties (e.g., "hot", "heavy"), even if they are the defining, highly salient characteristics of a vehicle like "fire" or "boulder." Instead, the pipeline must calculate a Local Salience Score for each of the 35k properties strictly relative to their assigned synsets. This can be operationalized by prompting the LLM to output a confidence or salience weight (0.0 to 1.0) for each extracted property during the initial data generation phase. The computational scoring function for a metaphor pair (A, B) must then weight the intersection f(A \cap B) exclusively by the salience of those properties in the vehicle (B), entirely ignoring their salience in the target (A). The revised metric should be: Score(A,B) = \sum_{p \in (A \cap B)} Salience_B(p). Furthermore, the system must penalize pairs where the shared property is already highly salient in the target, as this indicates literal similarity rather than a creative metaphorical leap.

3. Cross-Domain Semantic Similarity and Transfer
The current pipeline enforces cross-domain separation using a simple FastText cosine distance threshold. While this prevents literal synonyms from being proposed as metaphors (e.g., suggesting "rage" as a vehicle for "anger"), it remains a blunt instrument. Metaphors are essentially analogies functioning across domains (A:B :: C:D), requiring the computation of relational, rather than purely attributional, similarity.

	Latent Relational Analysis (LRA) (Turney, 2005/2006, Computational Linguistics)
	Critical
	Distinguishing attributional similarity from relational similarity by mapping the frequencies of connecting lexical patterns smoothed via SVD.
	Shifting the LLM extraction logic from flat properties to relational triples to enable analogical mapping across domains.
	https://web-archive.southampton.ac.uk/cogprints.org/5098/1/NRC-48775.pdf
	Peter Turney’s Latent Relational Analysis (LRA) draws a strict mathematical and theoretical distinction between attributional similarity (synonymy, calculated between two words) and relational similarity (analogy, calculated between two word pairs). LRA represents the relation between a pair of words as a vector of frequencies of connecting lexical patterns found in a massive corpus of unstructured text. LRA smooths this frequency matrix using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) and utilizes synonyms to dynamically expand the feature space, allowing the system to achieve human-level performance on SAT analogy questions.
The existing Metaforge pipeline operates entirely on attributional similarity, blindly matching properties of Word A with properties of Word B. To generate profound metaphors, the engine must incorporate relational similarity. Instead of merely finding a vehicle that shares static properties with the target, the system should identify relations that the target participates in, and find a vehicle that participates in parallel relations. If the LLM extraction pipeline is modified to extract relational triples (e.g., ) rather than flat boolean properties (e.g., `[destructive]`), Turney's LRA methodology can be directly applied. The pipeline would construct relational vectors for candidate vehicles (e.g., ) and calculate the cosine similarity between the SVD-smoothed relational vectors. This fundamental shift moves the engine from a simplistic "bag-of-properties" approach to genuine analogical reasoning.

	Structure Mapping Engine (SME) (Falkenhainer, Forbus, & Gentner, 1989)
	High
	Operationalizing Structure-Mapping Theory by finding isomorphic subgraphs between the knowledge graphs of two domains.
	Upgrading the 35k flat vocabulary into a lightweight ontology requiring typed property alignments.
	https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/GoldstoneMedinGentner91.pdf
	The Structure Mapping Engine (SME) operationalizes Dedre Gentner's Structure-Mapping Theory, arguably the most influential cognitive theory of analogy and metaphor. SME takes knowledge graphs representing the objects and relations of two distinct domains and attempts to find isomorphic subgraphs that yield structural alignments. SME strictly prioritizes deep relational matches over simple object-attribute matches.
The SME literature explicitly warns against the limitations of flat feature vectors. The failure of the Metaforge synonym clustering experiment is perfectly predicted by SME principles: when structural relations are ignored and flattened, the semantic space degrades into a soup of disconnected attributes, and deduplication destroys whatever latent structural signal remained. The pipeline's curated 35k vocabulary should be upgraded into a lightweight ontology. Properties should be explicitly typed (e.g., Action, PhysicalState, Origin). Matching should require that not only do the properties overlap, but the types of properties overlap in a structurally consistent manner. A significant scoring bonus should be applied when a cluster of interdependent properties in the target aligns with a functionally identical cluster in the vehicle.
4. Property and Feature Norms in NLP
The foundation of the entire Metaforge pipeline is its LLM-extracted semantic properties. To optimize downstream matching, the nature and quality of these extracted properties must be rigorously aligned with how human cognition categorizes normative features.

	McRae Feature Norms / CSLB (McRae et al., 2005)
	High
	Utilizing human-elicited psycholinguistic property norms as ground truth for semantic representation.
	Calibrating the LLM extraction prompts against the McRae database to mitigate severe reporting bias.
	https://aclanthology.org/S18-1117.pdf
	The McRae feature norms database is the standard psycholinguistic resource containing human-elicited properties for concepts (e.g., a duck "has webbed feet," "quacks"). Computational research has consistently shown that models trained on or correlated with McRae norms outperform standard distributional word embeddings (like Word2Vec) on specific cognitive and semantic similarity tasks.
A critical vulnerability in the current pipeline is the reliance on uncalibrated LLMs for property extraction. LLMs are known to suffer from massive reporting bias; they frequently fail to extract the most obvious, highly salient physical properties of objects because those properties are so foundational they are rarely stated explicitly in training text (e.g., texts rarely state "apples are solid" or "fire is hot"). To evaluate the quality of the 11 properties extracted per synset, the pipeline should cross-reference a random subset of its LLM-extracted features against the McRae feature norms dataset. If the LLM is primarily extracting abstract, distributional context rather than highly salient, ground-truth physical properties, the property generation prompt must be aggressively engineered to explicitly demand sensory, functional, and taxonomic features. Metaphor relies heavily on physical grounding; if the LLM is omitting physical norms, the downstream matching algorithm has no raw material to compute the salience imbalance required by Ortony's model.
It is equally important to acknowledge negative results within feature representation literature. Research analyzing discourse-level features surrounding metaphorical texts found that broader discourse properties are generally not indicative of metaphorical usage compared to local literal synonymous expressions. This confirms that the pipeline should maintain its focus on concept-level features rather than attempting to extract broad, multi-sentence contextual embeddings to score the synsets.
5. Evaluation Methods and Datasets
The current evaluation methodology—relying exclusively on Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) against a gold standard of 274 known metaphor pairs—is systematically suppressing the pipeline's apparent performance and blinding the developers to generative success. Metaphor generation is a highly generative, one-to-many task; there are thousands of valid, highly creative vehicles for the concept of "anger." A low MRR likely indicates the system is generating novel, apt metaphors that are simply absent from the highly restrictive 274-pair gold standard.

	MuseScorer: Idea Originality Scoring at Scale (Bangash et al., 2025, EMNLP)
	Critical
	Addressing the long-tail problem in creativity scoring using an LLM-as-a-judge framework with an externally orchestrated K-NN retrieval codebook.
	Replacing static vocabulary clustering with dynamic, intent-sensitive bucket creation to preserve the fat-tailed distribution of creative metaphors.
	https://aclanthology.org/2025.emnlp-main.1009.pdf
	MuseScorer provides a psychometrically validated system specifically designed for scoring the originality of ideas at scale, directly addressing the "long-tail" problem. Traditional clustering algorithms fail catastrophically on long-tail creative tasks because they force unique ideas into generalized buckets, destroying their statistical infrequency and distinctiveness. MuseScorer operates using an LLM-as-a-judge framework coupled with externally orchestrated K-NN retrieval. For every new idea generated, it retrieves the closest existing "buckets" from a dynamic codebook utilizing sentence embeddings (e.g., e5-large-v2). The LLM then judges if the idea genuinely fits an existing bucket; if it does not, a entirely new bucket is instantiated. This methodology rigorously preserves the fat-tailed distribution characteristic of human creativity.
The failed clustering experiment in the current pipeline attempted to squash the long tail of 35k properties into fewer buckets, which predictably destroyed performance. The pipeline must reverse this strategy entirely. Implement a dynamic codebook mechanism modeled directly after MuseScorer. When evaluating the uniqueness or novelty of a generated metaphor, use K-NN to compare it against a massive, dynamic database of known metaphors. If it falls into a high-frequency bucket, it receives a low novelty score. If it occupies a singleton or low-frequency bucket but maintains high salience intersection (as scored by the Ortony metric), it represents a highly creative, novel metaphor and should receive a massive scoring boost.

To rescue the MRR and guarantee the generation of profoundly apt metaphors, the composite score must be enriched with linguistic, statistical, and psycholinguistic signals that naturally govern human metaphor production and comprehension.

	Selectional Preference Violation (SPV) (Wilks, 1978; Resnik, 1993; Shutova, 2010)
	Critical
	Quantifying metaphoricity by measuring the statistical violation of typical word co-occurrences utilizing Selectional Association (SA).
	Integrating a dependency parser to calculate SA scores, rewarding mappings that structurally violate selectional norms.
	https://aclanthology.org/W16-1102.pdf
	Selectional Preference Violation (SPV) is one of the most robust and historically validated computational indicators of metaphoricity. It operates on the core linguistic principle that metaphors intentionally violate the statistical typicality of word co-occurrences. For example, the verb "drink" has a strong selectional preference for liquid nouns in its object position. The phrase "drink the knowledge" heavily violates this preference, signaling a metaphor.
SPV is computationally quantified using the Selectional Association (SA) metric, originally proposed by Resnik. It calculates how typical a noun (n) is for a given verb (v), normalized by the verb's overall Selectional Preference Strength (SPS):
A low SA score indicates a high degree of violation, and thus a high likelihood of metaphoricity.
The current pipeline generates metaphorical vehicles perfectly in isolation, but lacks any mechanism to verify if those vehicles can elegantly and grammatically map to the target's verbs or adjectives in natural language generation. The scoring function must integrate an SPV matrix. Using a large dependency-parsed corpus (e.g., Wikipedia), the system should pre-calculate the Selectional Association between the generated vehicle and the common verbs associated with the target domain. An ideal metaphorical pair will exhibit high property overlap (based strictly on Ortony's vehicle salience) combined with a highly negative Selectional Association (indicating a strong, deliberate semantic violation). This combination mathematically guarantees that the metaphor is both structurally sound and linguistically novel, circumventing the vulnerability of SPV metrics over-classifying highly frequent literal phrases.

	Concreteness and Imageability Differentials (Turney et al., 2011; Tsvetkov et al., 2014)
	High
	Utilizing psycholinguistic norms to enforce the directionality of metaphor (mapping abstract concepts to concrete sensory representations).
	Implementing a Concreteness Differential penalty utilizing the MRC Psycholinguistic database to prevent abstract-to-abstract mappings.
	https://aclanthology.org/W18-3028.pdf
	Metaphor mapping is overwhelmingly directional: it functions by mapping highly concrete source domains (vehicles) onto abstract target domains (tenors) to render the abstract comprehensible. Psycholinguistic metrics such as Concreteness (how tangible an object is to the physical senses) and Imageability (how easily a concept evokes a distinct mental picture) are massive, fundamental predictors of metaphor directionality. Computational models trained on resources like the MRC Psycholinguistic Database demonstrate universally that target words in metaphors consistently possess lower concreteness scores than their corresponding vehicles.
The current pipeline's reliance on a FastText domain distance threshold ensures the mapped words are semantically different, but it entirely fails to enforce this vital directionality. It is highly probable the system is currently suggesting abstract vehicles for concrete targets (e.g., suggesting "anger" as a vehicle for "fire"), which generates stylistically anomalous and cognitively dissonant results. A strict Concreteness Differential must be added to the scoring composite. Every synset in the database should be assigned a concreteness score (extrapolated via supervised learning algorithms on vector space representations from the MRC database, achieving precision of 0.94 as demonstrated by Tsvetkov ). The scoring function must heavily penalize any mapping where the target is more concrete than the vehicle: Concreteness(Target) \ge Concreteness(Vehicle).
